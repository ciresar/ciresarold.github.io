3. Maximum Likelihood Estimation (MLE)

Why is Maximum Likelihood Estimation Useful?

Many problems in microeconometrics have data that do not obey classical assumptions

  • Integer or dichotomous dependent variables
  • Censored or truncated variables
  • Stronger distributional assumptions are needed to estimate parameters of the underlying (latent) model from such data
    • Assumption of normality and use of MLE helps

MLE are asymptotically efficient:

  • Consistent, asymptotically normal and covariance matrix is smaller than cov matrix of any other consistent, asymptotically-normal estimator
  • So if we can show that an estimator is an MLE or has the properties of an MLE, it is also showing that this estimator is the most efficient

Special case: Maximum Likelihood Estimation of the Linear Model (aka "normal" regression)

CLRM assumptions:

Parameter estimates for $\beta$ can be derived using the following estimation principles:

  • Method of least squares - select the best estimator by minimizing the sum of the squared errors for the whole sample
  • Method of moments - equate the moments (mean, variance, etc.) implied by a statistical model of the population distribution with the actual moments observed in the sample and solve for the unknown parameter(s) $\theta$
  • Method of maximum likelihood (aka "normal regression") - data is drawn from some population distribution (e.g., normal) with unknown parameter(s) $\theta$. Select estimate of $\theta$ so as to maximize the likelihood of seeing the sample actually observed

All lead to the same estimators.

4. Generalized Least Square Estimator (GLS)

Because of omitted variables, we end up with a situation where the errors are correlated: $$ \mathbf{y}=X \boldsymbol{\beta}+\varepsilon $$ where $\mathbb{E}[\boldsymbol{\varepsilon} \mid X]=\mathbf{0}$ and $\operatorname{var}[\boldsymbol{\varepsilon} \mid X]=\mathbb{E}\left[\varepsilon \varepsilon^{\prime} \mid X\right]=\sigma^2\Omega$.

The consequence is that $\operatorname{var}\left[\hat\beta_{O L S} \mid X\right]\neq \sigma^{2}\left(X^{\prime} X\right)^{-1}$ $$ \begin{aligned} \operatorname{var}\left[\hat\beta_{O L S} \mid X\right] &=\mathbb{E}\left[\left(\hat\beta_{O L S}-\mathbb{E}\left[\hat\beta_{O L S} \mid X\right]\right)\left(\hat\beta_{O L S}-\mathbb{E}\left[\hat\beta_{O L S} \mid X\right]\right)^{\prime} \mid X\right] \\ &=\mathbb{E}\left[\left(X^{\prime} X\right)^{-1} X^{\prime} \varepsilon \varepsilon^{\prime} X\left(X^{\prime} X\right)^{-1} \mid X\right] \\ &=\left(X^{\prime} X\right)^{-1} X^{\prime} \mathbb{E}\left[\varepsilon \varepsilon^{\prime} \mid X\right] X\left(X^{\prime} X\right)^{-1} \\ &=\left(X^{\prime} X\right)^{-1} X^{\prime}\sigma^2\Omega X\left(X^{\prime} X\right)^{-1} \end{aligned} $$

The basic idea behind GLS is to transform the observation matrix $[\mathrm{y} \, \mathrm{X}]$ so that the variance in the transformed model is $\sigma^2 \mathrm{I}$ instead of $\sigma^2\Omega$.

Since $\boldsymbol{\Omega}$ is positive definite, its inverse is positive definite. Thus it is possible to find a nonsingular matrix $\boldsymbol{P}$ such that $$ \mathbf{\Omega}^{-1}=\boldsymbol{P}^{\prime} \boldsymbol{P} $$

NOTE: Matrix P is not a projection matrix!! I'm just following Prof. Nandi's notation.

Typical matrix decompositions used here are:

  • the Cholesky decomposition $\Omega^{-1} = \boldsymbol{LL'}$ where $\boldsymbol L$ is lower triangular
  • or the eigendecomposition $\Omega^{-1} = Q \Lambda Q' = Q \Lambda^{-1/2}\Lambda^{-1/2} Q^{\prime}$
